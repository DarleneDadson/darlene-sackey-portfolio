Gender Workplace Discrimination

As a result of women being a part of a wide range of professions over the past two centuries, society has become more diverse and productive in the labour force. However despite their growing presence in the labor force, women still encounter significant barriers to career advancement and fair treatment in the workplace. Systematic and societal discrimination perpetuate false gender assumptions and biases, resulting in limited promotions, unequal pay, harassment, and stereotyping.

Discrimination against women in the workplace is an issue that has been prevalent for centuries. Despite progress in gender equality, women continue to face barriers to advancement in their careers. From wage gaps to limited opportunities for promotion, women are often treated unfairly and discriminated against in the workplace. Manuela Barreto, a professor of psychology at the University of Exeter, has dedicated her research to understanding the nature and impact of such discrimination, particularly through the lens of the "glass ceiling" phenomenon. Her work sheds light on the challenges that women face in the workplace and the need for continued efforts to address and overcome gender-based discrimination.

The glass ceiling refers to the invisible barriers that prevent certain groups, often women and people of colour, from reaching the highest levels of their chosen profession. These barriers can take many forms, including lack of access to leadership positions, lower salaries, and limited opportunities for training and development. While progress has been made in recent years, the glass ceiling remains a significant challenge for many workers, particularly women.

One of the key findings of Barreto's research is that the glass ceiling is still a major barrier to women's advancement in the 21st century. Despite gains in gender equality in many areas, women continue to face significant obstacles in their careers. For example, women are often underrepresented in leadership positions, particularly in traditionally male-dominated fields such as science, technology, engineering, and mathematics.

Barreto's research has also highlighted the ways in which the glass ceiling affects women's perceptions of themselves and their abilities. Women who face these barriers may feel less confident in their skills and less likely to pursue opportunities for advancement. This, in turn, can perpetuate the cycle of inequality, as women are less likely to take on leadership roles and less likely to be seen as capable by others in the workplace.

Another important aspect of the glass ceiling, according to Barreto's research, is the role of unconscious bias. Unconscious biases are attitudes and beliefs that people hold without being aware of them, often based on stereotypes about certain groups. These biases can lead to discriminatory behavior, even among people who do not consider themselves prejudiced.

For example, a manager may unconsciously view women as less competent or less committed to their careers than men, even if they do not consciously hold these beliefs. This can lead to decisions that perpetuate the glass ceiling, such as choosing a less-qualified male candidate over a more qualified female candidate for a leadership position.

Barreto's research has also highlighted the importance of organizational culture in perpetuating or breaking down the glass ceiling. Organizational culture refers to the shared values, beliefs, and practices of a workplace. When an organization's culture is inclusive and supportive of diversity, it can help to break down the barriers of the glass ceiling. Conversely, when an organization's culture is exclusive or discriminatory, it can perpetuate the cycle of inequality.

One important way that organizations can address the glass ceiling is by implementing policies and practices that support gender equality. This might include initiatives such as mentoring programs, flexible work arrangements, and gender pay equity measures. By actively working to break down the barriers of the glass ceiling, organizations can create a more inclusive and equitable workplace for all employees.

Finally, Barreto's research has highlighted the importance of addressing the intersectionality of discrimination in the workplace. Intersectionality refers to the ways in which different forms of discrimination, such as those based on gender, race, and sexual orientation, can intersect and compound one another. For example, a woman of colour may face greater barriers to advancement than a white woman due to the added challenges of racial discrimination.

By acknowledging and addressing the intersectionality of discrimination, organizations can create more inclusive workplaces that support the advancement of all employees, regardless of their backgrounds.
